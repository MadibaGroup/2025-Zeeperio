\section{Introduction}

Decades of research on end-to-end verifiable (E2E-V) elections have shown strong cryptographic guarantees can be realized for protecting the integrity of elections and the secrecy of voter ballots~\cite{HR16}. In more recent years, pilot projects and deployments of E2E-V platforms have also demonstrated that these systems are usable and scalable to an appropriate degree~\cite{AMPQ09,CCC+10,BBB+13,CRST15}. Despite these efforts, some governments continue to run elections without meaningful verification or openness to scrutiny, and where the voter ballot selections and their identities exist in combination within the election service (see e.g., Cardillo et al. \cite{cae19}).

In this paper, we contribute to a broader discussion the cryptographic voting community has been having for some number of years: do we compromise our ideals and offer watered-down systems that the industry might find more palatable if they offer tangible security advantages over the status quo? We relate this to the term realpolitik, describing statecraft based on pragmatic calculation rather than ideology, and propose \textit{realcryptik} to describe a similar pragmatic approach in the election verification context.

Flavours of realcryptik already permeate the research literature. Despite opposition to internet voting, Helios~\cite{Adi08} is perhaps the most used E2E-V voting technology. While researchers argue coercion resistance is essential for internet voting, Helios provides none. Scantegrity II~\cite{CCC+08} was the first E2E-V system to see governmental use. It is built around the compromise that in a paper ballot system, it is acceptable for optical scanner to learn how the voter voted, side-stepping the complex ballot mechanics its predecessors to avoid this. In recent years, serious discussions have arose around whether E2E-V should be largely abandoned in favor of risk limiting audits? Should we affirm the use of ballot marking devices when we know most voters do not carefully check their output?

We propose a different compromise in this paper: Allow election service providers (vendors) to continue the practice of collecting unencrypted ballot selections, while introducing an measure of verifiability. For the election authorities (EAs), our approach continues to rely on legal protections implemented through procedures, i.e., without formal cryptographic secrecy protections and guarantees.

The research question of this paper is how to modify the cryptography of an E2E-V voting system to provide integrity, without the need to maintain ballot secrecy from the EA (but still provide receipt-freeness otherwise). The simpler threat model means we can streamline the cryptography, in particular we can prove that encrypted (or committed in our case) ballots are tallied correctly rather than computing the tally under encryption. This shifts the protocol away from multiparty computation and threshold cryptography to essentially an all encompassing zero knowledge proof (ZKP). While ZKPs have been used in voting systems from almost their invention in 1980s and onward, we focus on modern succinct ZKPs called zk-SNARKs.

\paragraph{Zeeperio.}

Zeeperio is a modernization of the Eperio E2E-V backend~\cite{ECHA10}. Eperio was an attempt to simplify the cryptography of Scantegrity II by making larger, aggregated commitments that could be implemented with file-based encryption toward more practical implementations of the proof verification using pre-existing and commonplace software. The paper, for example, demonstrated an implementation of the verifier using only the in-built features of Microsoft Excel. Eperio's security was studied in detail using formal methods at CCS 2021 paper~\cite{BDGR21}. Like Scantegrity (and related schemes of the era), Eperio still required election authorities (EAs) to organize an independent and explicit proof-checking effort to ensure {\em someone} checked the proof at all.

If, however, verification could be reduced to a succinct (zk-SNARK) argument, voters and candidates can still independently verify the election (if they choose), but the proof could now be automatically checked in a smart contract, giving EAs the automatic assurance that at least {\em someone} checked it. In this paper, that someone is the Ethereum network, which connects the acceptability of the election proof to the network's \$100+B USD market capitalization. The design challenge of this work is to fit the proof within the highly restricted computational limits of the smart contract setting.

\subsection{Contribution.}

Zeeperio is an update to the Eperio election-verification approach, replacing antiquated cut-and-choose full-open commitments with modern, succinct knowledge arguments toward automated verification in a smart-contract setting.

We assume the reader is already familiar with end-to-end verifiable (E2E-V) voting protocols which allow for: (i) an election authority to prove an election tally is computed correctly without learning individual voter choices, and for (ii) voters to check their individual ballots are included unmodified in the tally without being able to reveal how they voted to anyone else. Eperio is an E2E-V tallying protocol (or `backend') that composes with several styles of paper ballots, including Pret a Voter and Scantegrity I. A brief description of Eperio is provided in Figure~\ref{fig:eperio} with a Pret a Voter style ballot.

In addition to succinctness, we also clean up a few issues that were not fully resolved in Scantegrity II and Eperio:

\begin{itemize}

\item As pointed about by Basin \etal~\cite{BDGR21}, Eperio does not prove ballots were not overvoted; Zeeperio does (\S\ref{sec:tally}).
\item As acknowledged by Essex \etal~\cite{ECHA10}, Eperio does not support Scantegrity II ballots; Zeeperio does (\S\ref{sec:receipt}).
\item As acknowledged by Chaum \etal~\cite{CCC+08}, Scantegrity II supports dispute resolution but it requires all confirmation codes on the ballot to be revealed. Zeeprio offers a simple proof of incorrect code without revealing information about all codes (\S\ref{sec:dispute}).

\end{itemize}

A remaining issue, pointed out by Basin et al~\cite{BDGR21}, are clash attacks~\cite{KTV12} which are not explicitly addressed by Eperio. However they are applicable to all major E2E-V voting systems and are addressed through strict polling place policies that denote which ballot is given to which voter.

\subsection{Succinct Proofs}

While zero knowledge proofs/arguments (ZKPs) date back to the 1980s~\cite{GMW86,BCC88}, classic ZKPs produce proofs that are at least linear in the size of the statement being proven. By contrast, succinct zero knowledge arguments (SNARKs) are sublinear, typically with a logarithmic or constant proof size. A Polynomial Interactive Oracle Proof (PIOPs) is a pattern that several SNARKs have in common~\cite{MBKM19,CHM+20,BFS20}. In this pattern, the prover specifies polynomials and verifiers challenge the prover to open these polynomials at random points. PIOP does not say \textit{how} to make a SNARK; it is merely a generic pattern. 

To realize a PIOP, the prover must send a succinct representation of each polynomial from the prover to the verifier, which commits the prover to those polynomials. When opening a polynomial, the verifier must be certain it is the opening of the same polynomial that was committed to. PIOPs rely on a cryptographic primitive called a polynomial commitment scheme (PCS) to handle this. The rest of a PIOP protocol does not use any cryptography. A number of PCSs have been proposed, the most prominent are KZG (very short, based on bilinear pairings, requires a trusted setup) and (DEEP-)FRI (longer, based on hash functions, conjectured to be post-quantum secure, no trusted setup). In Zeeperio, we use KZG~\cite{KZG10} because it integrates well with Ethereum.


KZG uses a universal trusted setup: a secret value $\tau$ is chosen at random, used to compute a structured reference string (SRS) $\tuple{g^\tau, g^{\tau^2},g^{\tau^3},\ldots}$ in a pairing-friendly group defined by generator $g$, and then erased. Knowledge of $\tau$ after the setup is sufficient to fake KZG openings and thus forge false ZKP transcripts. However the setup can be run as an open participation multiparty computation where participants prove their contribution is correct and only one honest participant must forget $\tau$ for the SRS to be secure. Ethereum ran an open trusted setup in 2023, accepting 100K contributions over 208 days. A single SRS, such as this, can be shared by everyone wanting to do a KZG commitment (known as a universal trusted setup). 

In practice, zk-SNARKs for a specific computation can be deployed in different ways: (a) written in a high-level language and compiled to VM instructions, (b) written in special markup languages and compiled into arithmetic circuits, or (c) written as a custom arithmetic circuit. In each case, these representations are fed into a general-purpose proof system for circuits or zkVMs. Each level of abstraction adds overhead and costs to the prover. Our approach is `closer to the metal.' We do not use circuits or Plonk itself. We instead go underneath what Plonk provides and use the same kind of building blocks it uses to make a custom PIOP argument for Zeeperio.

Zeeperio follows the conventional paradigm in which arbitrary relations are reduced to algebraic constraints and proven succinctly when viewed through the lens of general-purpose zero-knowledge proof systems (GPZKPs) \cite{cryptoeprint:2024/1902}.
Nevertheless, we improves on this design by bypassing the circuit/zkVM abstraction entirely and building polynomial identities directly over the election data.
Furthermore, rather than adopting transparent proof systems such as FRI or STARKs that eliminate trusted setup at the expense of larger proofs and higher prover complexity, Zeeperio uses a universal KZG setup to achieve constant-size proofs and efficient on-chain verification \cite{10.1007/978-3-031-69231-4_4}.

\subsection{Smart Contracts}

Smart contracts are programs deployed on a blockchain, such as Ethereum. They only execute when a user calls a function. The blockchain's consensus enforces that the function call executes correctly, and the state of the code is updated accordingly. In Zeeprio, a smart contract will accept an election proof and run the verification of it, reporting if it is correct or incorrect. Since smart contracts can implement arbitrary code, they can support cryptographic operations however this approach is usually prohibitively expensive. \paragraph{Parameters.}
Let $\mathcal{B}$ be the number of printed ballots and $\mathcal{C}$ the number of candidates per ballot. Let $\mathcal{P}=\mathcal{B}\mathcal{C}$ be the number of ballot positions. Let $n$ be the padded column length (a power of two) with $n\ge \mathcal{P}$ (the need is explained below). Let $\mathbb{F}_q$ be a finite field of size $q$. A ballot has ID $b\in\{0,\dots,\mathcal{B}-1\}$ and occupies a \emph{block}: $I_b=\{\,b\mathcal{C},\,b\mathcal{C}+1,\,\dots,\,b\mathcal{C}+(\mathcal{C}-1)\,\}\subseteq\{0,\dots,n-1\}.$

\paragraph{Walkthrough of a constraint.} 

Notice from Figure~\ref{fig:eperio}, the mark column can only contain a 0 or 1. This is one small fact about what constitutes a valid election table. With ZKPs, we call this a \textit{constraint}. Zeeperio consists of \constraints similar constraints that all must hold in any valid election table. Here, we will demonstrate a complete argument for this single constraint (which we denote as c2.2 later) to demonstrate how a PIOP works. This constraint is adapted from~\cite{BFGW19}. 

The mark column can be considered a one-dimensional vector or array $A_\mathsf{mark}[i]$ of length $\mathcal{P}$. Elements in the array will be encoded as elements of $\mathbb{F}_q$, the exponent field of a pairing-friendly elliptic curve such as \altbn or \bls. Cryptographically speaking, the array $A_\mathsf{mark}[i]$ could thus hold any integer in $[0,q-1]$ but the constraint says it is only a valid election table if it is further restricted to $[0,1]$.

To prove the constraint, the prover (election authority) will send to the verifier (smart contract) a succinct (one group element) commitment (hiding and binding) to the entire mark column $A_\mathsf{mark}[i]$. While vector commitments exist, in a PIOP, the prover commits to $A_\mathsf{mark}[i]$ indirectly by first encoding the data from $A_\mathsf{mark}[i]$ into a univariate polynomial over $\mathbb{F}_q$ and then commits to this polynomial representation of $A_\mathsf{mark}[i]$ using a polynomial commitment scheme such as KZG.

There are a variety of ways to encode vectors into polynomials, and we will focus on the method we will use. The prover chooses a set of x-coordinates, such as $\{0,1,2,3,\ldots\}$ which is called an evaluation domain $\mathcal{H}$. It then takes the data from $A_\mathsf{mark}[i]$ and forms a set of points $\{(0,A_\mathsf{mark}[0]),(1,A_\mathsf{mark}[1]),\ldots\}$. The prover interpolates a univariate polynomial through these points: $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$. It commits to $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$ using the polynomial commitment scheme, and sends the commitment to the verifier.

Interpolation is computationally expensive for provers, so they use an optimization. The evaluation domain is arbitrary and can be anything as long as the prover and verifier agree. Let $\omega\in\mathbb{F}_q$ generate a multiplicative subgroup of size $n$. Then set $H=\{\omega^0,\omega^1,\dots,\omega^{n-1}\}\subseteq \mathbb{F}_q$. Doing so offers three advantages and one drawback. Interpolating a polynomial over this domain can be accomplished in $\mathcal{O}(n \log n)$ instead of  $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$. Traversing the domain easy: the next index is the current index multiplied by $\omega$ and because it is closed under multiplication, the `next' element after the last element $\omega^{n-1}$ wraps back to the first element $\omega^{n-1}\cdot\omega=\omega^{n}=\omega^{0}$. Finally, a polynomial that is 0 at each $X\in H$ has a simple closed form: $\mathsf{Z}_H(X)=X^n-1$. The drawback is that the prover cannot use arrays of arbitrary length $\mathcal{P}$ and expect that an order $n$ subgroup will just exist. The subgroups are based on the parameters of the elliptic curve and curves like \altbn and \bls are designed to offer subgroups of sizes $\{2,2^2,2^3,2^4,\ldots\}$ up to $2^{12}$ for \altbn and $2^{32}$ for \bls.

In Zeeprio, if we have a column of length $\mathcal{P}$, we will zero pad it the nearest power of 2. Thus $n$ is the padded column length (a power of two) with $n\ge \mathcal{P}$. For this example constraint (the mark column is binary), the presence of zero padding does not affect the constraint. However, for other constraints, we will have to explicitly handle the padding separately from the election table data. 

At this point, the verifier has a polynomial commitment to $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$ which we will call $K_{\mathsf{Mark}}$. How does the prover demonstrate it is binary? The prover will construct another polynomial as follows: $V(X)\ :=\ \mathsf{Mark}(X)\bigl(\mathsf{Mark}(X)-1\bigr)$. It will commit to $V(X)$ and send $K_{V}$ to the verifier. If $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$ is binary for all $X\in H$, then $V(X)$ will be zero for all $X\in H$. If $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$ is not binary for at least one $X\in H$, $V(X)$ will be non-zero at the same $X$. We have converted the constraint $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$ is binary into: 

\begin{enumerate}
\item $V(X)$ is correctly constructed from $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$
\item  $V(X)$ it is zero for all $X\in H$. A polynomial that is is zero for all $X\in H$ is called a \textit{vanishing polynomial} (on $H$).
\end{enumerate}

To prove that $V(X)$ is correctly constructed from $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$, the verifier has $K_{\mathsf{Mark}}$ and $K_{V}$. The verifier will (or the prover using the strong Fiat-Shamir heuristic) choose a random evaluation point $\zeta \notin H$. Using the polynomial commitment scheme, the prover will open the polynomials at the value $\zeta$ sending $V(\zeta)$, $\mathsf{Mark}(\zeta)$, and opening proofs for each to the verifier. The verifier will check the opening proofs and check that $V(\zeta)\ :=\ \mathsf{Mark}(\zeta)\bigl(\mathsf{Mark}(\zeta)-1\bigr)$. Just because the relation holds at the point $\zeta$, what does that say about it holding at each $X\in H$? The answer is that it overwhelmingly holds due to the Schwartz-Zippel lemma, which we will not review further here.

Next, the prover will demonstrate that $V(X)$ vanishes on $H$. Recall that $\mathsf{Z}_H(X)=X^n-1$ is a vanishing polynomial on $H$. The prover will construct another polynomial $Q(X):=V(X)/\mathsf{Z}_H(X)$, commits to $Q(X)$, and sends the commitment $K_{Q}$ to the verifier. If and only if $V(X)$ is zero at every point of $H$, $\mathsf{Z}_H(X)$ exactly divides $V(X)$ and results in a proper polynomial $Q(X)$, otherwise $Q(X)$ will be a rational function not reducible to a polynomial. $Q(X):=V(X)/\mathsf{Z}_H(X)$ can be rearranged as $V(X)-Q(X)\cdot(X^n-1)=0$. Using $\zeta$ from above, the prover sends $Q(\zeta)$ and its opening proof. The verifier checks $V(\zeta)-Q(\zeta)\cdot(\zeta^n-1)=0$. By Schwartz–Zippel, if this equality holds at a uniformly random $\zeta\notin H$, then except with negligible probability it holds as a polynomial identity, which implies that $V(X)$ vanishes on $H$. \qed


\subsection{Zeeperio Constraints}

Zeeperio is made up of \constraints constraints. Each constraint follows a similar pattern to the example constraint: a set of polynomials is committed to such that a relationship between them will be a vanishing polynomial if and only if the constraint is true. Once the constraint is converted into a vanishing polynomial, proving the vanishing polynomial is constructed correctly and that it actually vanishes is identical to the process used above. 

In fact, given a set of vanishing polynomials $\{V_1(X),V_2(X),\ldots,V_m(X)\}$ and their corresponding quotient polynomials, the check can be batched into a single check. The prover will form a `super-polynomial' $\mathsf{Batch}(X)$ where each coefficient is one of the vanishing polynomials:

$$
\mathsf{Batch}(Y)=\sum_{i=1}^{m}Y^{i}\Bigl(V_i(X)-Q_i(X)\,\mathsf{Z}_H(X)\Bigr)
$$

A polynomial where every coefficient is 0 is a zero-polynomial. Therefore the verifier (or prover via strong Fiat-Shamir) will choose a random $\zeta\in \mathbb{F}_q\notin H$ as before and set $X=\zeta$, which overwhelmingly makes every coefficient zero. To see if every coefficient is zero, it will also choose a random $\alpha\in\mathbb{F}_q$ and set $Y=\alpha$. If every $V_i(X)$ vanishes, then $\mathsf{Batch}(Y)$ is a zero polynomial and: 
$$
\mathsf{Batch}(\alpha)=\sum_{i=1}^{m}\alpha^{i}\Bigl(V_i(\zeta)-Q_i(\zeta)\,\mathsf{Z}_H(\zeta)\Bigr)=0.
$$

Zeeperio uses several public helper polynomials that depend only on the election `shape' (e.g., $n$, $\mathcal{B}$, $\mathcal{C}$, and hence $\mathcal{P}=\mathcal{B}\mathcal{C}$), and not on any voter choices or on the contents of the committed columns. This does not undermine the succinctness claims of Zeeprio: the pre-computation is offline, public, and independent of the witness. In systems like Plonk, these types of polynomials are considered as a `verifier's key.' In practice, election administrators can standardize on a small set of template sizes, and these polynomials can be pre-computed once for each election template. If an election uses fewer than the template's maximum number of ballots or candidates, the remaining rows are constrained to be empty: extra ballots are unused and extra candidates receive zero votes, which is directly reflected in the published tally.

\subsection{Election Specific Polynomials}

The Eperio tables for an election consist of three (padded) columns, which will be encoded into polynomials evaluated over $H$. We call the polynomials $\mathsf{Code}(X)$, $\mathsf{Audit}(X)$, and $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$. How each index maps to a unique ballot ID and candidate is provided as a public template, and columns will be sorted by ballot ID and then candidate (see Figure~\ref{fig:eperio}). The final tally is a vector $\mathsf{Tally}[0..\mathcal{C}-1]\in\mathbb{F}_q^{\mathcal{C}}$.

Across all the constraints, we will use an additional 5 `helper' polynomials. We will introduce them as needed, however they consist of two (additive, suffix) accumulator polynomials: $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)$ and $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Mark}}(X)$, where $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(\omega^{i}) \;=\; \sum_{t=i}^{n-1} \mathsf{Audit}(\omega^{t})$; likewise for $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$. We also define a `stridded' accumulator $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Vote}}(X)$ that we will describe with constraints 4. Finally we also define two block sum helper polynomials: $\mathsf{BlkSum}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)=\sum_{k=0}^{\mathcal{C}-1}\mathsf{Audit}(\omega^k X)$ and $\mathsf{BlkSum}_{\mathsf{Mark}}(X)=\sum_{k=0}^{\mathcal{C}-1}\mathsf{Mark}(\omega^k X)$.

\subsection{Audit Constraints}
\label{sec:audit}

Due to limited space in the paper, we cannot fully explain each of the \constraints constraints. Instead we provide some intuition for them.

\begin{mdframed}[linewidth=0.6pt,backgroundcolor=verylightpink,innerleftmargin=6pt,innerrightmargin=6pt,innertopmargin=4pt,innerbottommargin=4pt]
\[
\begin{aligned}
V_{c1.1}(X)\ &:=\ \mathsf{Audit}(X)\,\mathsf{Z}_{\mathsf{head}}(X)\\
V_{c1.2}(X)\ &:=\ \mathsf{Audit}(X)\bigl(\mathsf{Audit}(X)-1\bigr) \\
V_{c1.3}(X)\ &:=\ \bigl(\mathsf{Sel}_{\mathsf{blk}}(X)\mathsf{BlkSum}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)\bigr)\bigl(\mathsf{Sel}_{\mathsf{blk}}(X)\mathsf{BlkSum}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)-\mathcal{C}\bigr) \\
V_{c1.4}(X)\ &:=\ \bigl(\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)-\mathsf{Audit}(X)+\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(\omega X)\bigr)\cdot(X-\omega^{n-1})\\
V_{c1.5}(X)\ &:=\ \bigl(\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)-\mathsf{Audit}(X)\bigr)\cdot \mathsf{Mask}_{n-1}(X)\\
V_{c1.6}(X)\ &:=\ \bigl(\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)-\mathsf{Sum}_{\mathsf{Audit}\times\mathcal{C}}\bigr)\cdot \mathsf{Mask}_{0}(X)
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{mdframed}

The audit column is well-defined for an election if all padding bits have the value 0 (c1.1) and the column is otherwise binary (c1.2). Each ballot spans $\mathcal{C}$ positions, so the audit column should contain a contiguous block of $\mathcal{C}$ ones for an audited ballot, and a block of $\mathcal{C}$ zeros for unaudited ballots. The mechanism is to compute a block-sum over each ballot, $\mathsf{BlkSum}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)=\sum_{k=0}^{\mathcal{C}-1}\mathsf{Audit}(\omega^k X)$, then restrict attention to ballot-start indices using $\mathsf{Sel}_{\mathsf{blk}}(X)$. For a well-formed audit column, the filtered block-sum is always either $0$ (not audited) or $\mathcal{C}$ (audited), and never an intermediate value. Note that after choosing $\zeta$, in order to check $\mathsf{BlkSum}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)$ is well-formed, the verifier requires $\mathcal{C}$ opened points from $\mathsf{Audit}(\omega^0\zeta)$ to $\mathsf{Audit}(\omega^{\mathcal{C}-1}\zeta)$, so this check is linear in $\mathcal{C}$ (but more importantly constant in $\mathcal{P}$ and $n$).

The EA will assert the number of print audits in the election as the integer $\mathsf{Sum}_\mathsf{A}$. Since each print audit ballot contains a block of $\mathcal{C}$ 1's, the number of 1's in $A(X)$ must be $\mathsf{Sum}_{\mathsf{A}\times\mathcal{C}}=\mathsf{Sum}_\mathsf{A}\cdot\mathcal{C}$. To reason about the sum across a polynomial on $H$, many protocols use accumulator polynomials~\cite{GWC19,BFGW19}. $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)$ is intended to hold a running suffix-sum of the audit column down the domain order (including padding, which is forced to be $0$ by c1.1):

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(\omega^{i}) &:= \mathsf{Audit}(\omega^{i}) + \mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(\omega^{i+1}) \ \ \text{for } i=0,1,\ldots,n-2,\\
\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(\omega^{n-1}) &:= \mathsf{Audit}(\omega^{n-1}).
\end{aligned}
$$

Constraint c1.4 and c1.5 check each line of this recurrence respectively, guaranteeing that $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)$ is formed correctly. The first value in $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Audit}}(X)$ holds the sum, so c1.6 ensures this value is equal to $\mathsf{Sum}_{\mathsf{A}\times\mathcal{C}}$.

The constraints on the mark column parallel the constraints on the audit column. Padding is zero (c2.1), the column is binary (c2.2), and the total number of votes matches the sum announced by the election authority $\mathsf{Sum}_{\mathsf{Mark}}$ (c2.4--c2.5). The main difference is constraint c2.3. For each ballot in the audit column (previous section), the ballot's block is either all 0 or all 1, summing to either 0 or $\mathcal{C}$. For the vote column (this section), each ballot block is either all 0 (not voted) or contains a single 1 (voted). Since it is binary (c2.2), the EA can demonstrate that the sum of each ballot block is either 0 or 1, which is c2.3. We assume that over-voted ballots are rejected by the scanner or automatically converted into print audit ballots. 

\subsection{Cross-Column Constraints}

\[
\begin{aligned}
V_{c3.1}(X)\ :=\ \mathsf{Audit}(X)\,\mathsf{Mark}(X)

A final constraint demonstrates that no ballot is both voted and audited. This is simple: an unvoted ballot will have all 0's in its ballot block, while an unaudited ballot will have the same. Therefore, the Hadamard product (element-wise multiplication) of the audit column with the mark column will be 0 in every element if it is properly formed. In the malicious case, if a ballot is both voted and audited, at least one element of the ballot block will contain a 1 in both columns, and the product of the columns will not be 0 at that element, thus $V_{c3.1}(X)$ will not vanish. 

\subsection{Tally Constraints}
\label{sec:tally}

\begin{mdframed}[linewidth=0.6pt,backgroundcolor=verylightpink,innerleftmargin=6pt,innerrightmargin=6pt,innertopmargin=4pt,innerbottommargin=4pt]
\[
\begin{aligned}
V_{c4.1}(X)\ &:=\ \bigl(\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Vote}}(X)-\mathsf{Mark}(X)+\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Vote}}(\omega^{\mathcal{C}}X)\bigr)\cdot \mathsf{Mask}_{\mathsf{tail}}(X) \\
V_{c4.2}(X)\ &:=\ \bigl(\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Vote}}(X)-\mathsf{Mark}(X)\bigr)\cdot \frac{\mathsf{Z}_H(X)}{\mathsf{Mask}_{\mathsf{tail}}(X)}\\
V_{c4.3,j}(X)\ &:=\ \bigl(\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Vote}}(X)-\mathsf{Tally}[j]\bigr)\cdot \frac{\mathsf{Z}_H(X)}{X-\omega^{j}}
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{mdframed}

The EA will announce the tally as a list of vote totals for each of the $\mathcal{C}$ candidates: $\mathsf{Tally}[j]$ for $j\in\{0,\ldots,\mathcal{C}-1\}$. The marks for candidate $i$ will be in every $i$-th element of $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$ over $H$. The EA will build an additive, suffix accumulator $\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Vote}}(X)$ however it will be \textit{strided}: 

$$
\mathsf{Acc}_{\mathsf{Vote}}(\omega^i)\ :=\ \sum_{t=0}^{\left\lfloor\frac{n-1-i}{\mathcal{C}}\right\rfloor}\mathsf{Mark}\!\left(\omega^{\,i+t\mathcal{C}}\right),
\qquad i\in\{0,\ldots,n-1\}.
$$

The result is that $\mathsf{Acc}{\mathsf{Vote}}(\omega^c)$ is equal the total number of marks appearing at indices congruent to $c \bmod \mathcal{C}$ across all ballots (and padding). Note that there is no assumption that $c | n$; the sum for some candidates may be one element longer than others. Constraint c4.1 demonstrates the accumulator is built correctly, assuming it starts with the right values. Constraint c4.2 demonstrates that the starting values are correct. Constraint c4.3 demonstrates that the published tally matches the sum of each candidate's vote. Constraint c4.3 is run $\mathcal{C}$ times, once for each candidate's total votes, which will be contained in the first $\mathcal{C}$ elements of  $\mathsf{Acc}{\mathsf{Vote}}(X)$ on $H$.

\section{Voter Checks}
\label{sec:voter}
Eperio, like many voting systems, splits the E2E-V proof into two parts: a universal verifiable proof that the tally is correct, and a voter-verifiable proof that receipts are correct. All constraints from Section~\ref{sec:constraints} correspond to the former proof, whereas in this section we describe the voter-verifiable checks.

\subsection{Print Audit}
\label{sec:print}

The voter provides ballot ID $b$, which determines the public index set $I_b={b\mathcal{C},\ldots,b\mathcal{C}+\mathcal{C}-1}$. The EA opens the committed polynomials $\mathsf{Audit}(X)$, $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$, and $\mathsf{Code}(X)$ at the $\mathcal{C}$ points ${\omega^i}_{i\in I_b}$ and returns those evaluations together with opening proofs (for KZG, these can be batched in a single multipoint opening proof). 

\subsection{Receipt Check}
\label{sec:receipt}

At a first glance, it might seem the EA can simply open $\mathsf{Mark}(X)$ and $\mathsf{Code}(X)$ for the voter's ballot ID $b$. However, since these polynomials are ordered by candidate, revealing the index of the mark reveals who the voter voted for. We analyzed three PIOP-friendly approaches to this, but for space considerations, we present only the best: using a permutation (briefly, the excluded alternatives are lookup arguments and a secret selector vector with summation). 

First, the EA defines a public \emph{ballot-ID column} $\mathsf{BallotID}(X)$ that encodes the ballot identifier on every ballot position: for each ballot $b\in\{0,\dots,\mathcal{B}-1\}$ and each within-ballot offset $k\in\{0,\dots,\mathcal{C}-1\}$, we set $\mathsf{BallotID}(\omega^{b\mathcal{C}+k})=b$, and we set $\mathsf{BallotID}(\omega^i)=0$ on all padding indices $i\ge \mathcal{P}$. Next, the EA samples a secret permutation $\pi$ over $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$ and uses it to define permuted columns by $\mathsf{F}^{\pi}(\omega^i)=\mathsf{F}(\omega^{\pi(i)})$. The EA then commits once (pre-election or once-per-election) to the shuffled columns $\mathsf{BallotID}^{\pi}(X)$, $\mathsf{Code}^{\pi}(X)$, and $\mathsf{Mark}^{\pi}(X)$, and proves once that each shuffled column is a permutation of its corresponding source column under the \emph{same} secret permutation $\pi$. The permutation argument comes straight from the literature~\cite{GWC19}.\footnote{The only minor difference is that we are shuffling the random linear combination of three polynomials instead of two polynomials under the same permutation.}

For a per-receipt query, the voter provides ballot ID $b$. The EA finds, privately, an index $t$ such that $\mathsf{BallotID}^{\pi}(\omega^t)=b$ and $\mathsf{Mark}^{\pi}(\omega^t)=1$, and it returns the receipt code $c:=\mathsf{Code}^{\pi}(\omega^t)$ together with a batched opening proof at the single point $\omega^t$ for the three committed polynomials $\mathsf{BallotID}^{\pi}(X)$, $\mathsf{Mark}^{\pi}(X)$, and $\mathsf{Code}^{\pi}(X)$. Revealing the index $t$ is safe because $\pi$ is secret, so $t$ does not reveal the candidate-order position of the mark in the original (unshuffled) ballot block.

\subsection{Dispute Resolution}
\label{sec:dispute}

If a receipt check returns a wrong confirmation code, it is possible that the EA switched the voter's vote to another candidate. However, the voter has proof of this: they know a different confirmation code that appears on the ballot. By contrast, a voter who wants to discredit an election result might claim spuriously that their confirmation code is incorrect (even though it is); however, they will not know a different valid confirmation on their ballot (beyond guessing)~\cite{CCC+08}.

 In a dispute, the voter provides a ballot ID $b$ and a disputed confirmation code $c\in\mathbb{F}_q$. The EA’s goal is to prove \emph{non-membership}: that $c$ does not appear anywhere among the $\mathcal{C}$ confirmation codes printed on ballot $b$, without revealing any of those codes. Let $I_b=\{b\mathcal{C},\ldots,b\mathcal{C}+(\mathcal{C}-1)\}$ denote the (public) index set of ballot $b$, and let $\mathsf{Mask}^{(b)}_{\mathsf{ballot}}(X)$ be the public $0/1$ mask that is $1$ on $I_b$ and $0$ elsewhere on $H$. If $c$ is a non-member, then $(\mathsf{Code}(\omega^i)-c)$ will be non-zero on each element in $I_b$. If and only if it is non-zero, an inverse element will exist such that $(\mathsf{Code}(\omega^i)-c) \cdot \mathsf{Inv}_b(\omega^i)=1$. The EA introduces an auxiliary polynomial of asserted inverses $\mathsf{Inv}_b(X)$ on $I_b$ and the constraint reduces the vanishing polynomial: 

\begin{mdframed}[linewidth=0.6pt,backgroundcolor=verylightpink,innerleftmargin=6pt,innerrightmargin=6pt,innertopmargin=4pt,innerbottommargin=4pt]
\[
\begin{aligned}
V_{\mathsf{dispute}}(X)\ :=\ \bigl((\mathsf{Code}(X)-c)\cdot \mathsf{Inv}_b(X)-1\bigr)\cdot \mathsf{Mask}^{(b)}_{\mathsf{ballot}}(X)
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{mdframed}

If $c$ does appear on the ballot, say at position $\kappa\in I_b$, the EA will not be able to put a value in $\mathsf{Inv}_b(\kappa)$ that makes $V_{\mathsf{dispute}}(X)$ vanish. 

\section{Implementation and Performance}
Zeeperio is implemented as two separate components:
\paragraph{Off-chain Prover.}
The prover is implemented in Rust and uses the \emph{arkworks} toolkit for BN254 pairings. 
The instance is launched with a CLI that generates all three proofs described in Sections~\ref{sec:constraints} and~\ref{sec:voter}.
It commits to all polynomials using the \emph{hiding} variant of KZG, where each commitment includes a blinding term derived from the SRS. 
The prover also uses \emph{off-domain masking} where it randomizes witness polynomials with additional masking to prevent any information leakage.
To maximize cost efficiency, the prover batches all polynomial evaluations using a random scalar $\gamma$ into a single KZG opening proof.
This allows the verifier to check all evaluations simultaneously with a single pairing check.
Finally, the proofs are exported to calldata using Ethereum ABI encoding.
\paragraph{Onchain Verifier.}
The verifier smart contract is implemented in Solidity using the \texttt{Foundry} project setup. 
The contract takes an ABI-encoded proof containing the public inputs, polynomial commitments and opening proofs, 
and verifies them on-chain using the BN254 pairing precompiles. 
The verification logic is split into three functions: \texttt{verifyMain}, \texttt{verifyInclusion}, and \texttt{verifyReceipt},
corresponding to the three proofs described in Sections~\ref{sec:constraints} and~\ref{sec:voter}.
It should be noted that both prover and verifier recompute the same challenges, so they need to share
the same serialization rules.

\section{Concluding Remarks}

Zeeperio is a succinct proof of an Eperio-encoded election that can be autonomously verified by a smart contract. Even if the reader does not buy the notion of realcryptik, that not hiding raw ballots from the EA can be a stepping stone to full E2E-V elections, Zeeperio is still useful as a drop-in (or complimentary) mechanism for standard Eperio. Eperio (like Scantegrity and Punchscan) makes a `blackbox assumption' for ballot privacy that can be extended to Zeeperio. This leads to a natural adoption pathway for vendors and election officials: Zeeprio introduces initial an verifiability component with an automated argument checking via smart contracts as an initial stepping stone. Stronger ballot secrecy guarantees can later be integrated with TEE's for full E2E-V.

Describing the entirety of Zeeperio is challenging in the available page length. We note that a full version of this work, in the form of a thesis,\footnote{Link withheld for anonymity.} explains each constraint in detail, offers broader discussion of the implementation, and provides sketches of security arguments. 

Fortunately, the security analysis of Zeeperio makes effective use of existing analysis: Eperio has already been shown to satisfy end-to-end verifiability in both a cryptographic treatment~\cite{ECHA10} and a formal-methods treatment~\cite{BDGR21}. And, modern PIOP-based zk-SNARK constructions justify proving collections of algebraic constraints by reducing them to vanishing-polynomial checks~\cite{GWC19}. Therefore, the security question is relatively straightforward, reducing only to whether our specific vanishing constraints characterize the same relations that Eperio enforces.
